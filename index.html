About

Two decades of systems work, bottom-up: OS schedulers for many-core processors at UC Berkeley’s AMPLab, a zero-downtime schema evolution system as my PhD dissertation, and now the metadata and caching layer of Alluxio. The common thread is building systems designed to run correctly in production — not just to demonstrate an idea in a paper.

At Alluxio I lead as Engineering Manager and PMC member: metadata management, high-availability consensus (we migrated from ZooKeeper to Raft), and a next-generation query service targeting AI agentic workloads. The role spans both deep IC work and engineering management — I tend to drift toward whichever is the bottleneck.

The problems I find most interesting sit at the boundary between research and production: how compute-storage disaggregation behaves under real workloads, how AI is changing the contract between applications and data systems, and how large language models are reshaping the practice of software engineering itself. The work I do now sits in the middle of all three.

Featured Work

Ratchet — Automated Online Schema Evolution

My PhD dissertation system that lets database administrators perform efficient, zero-downtime schema evolution on PostgreSQL. Ratchet uses fine-grained locking and a delayed-copy strategy to migrate schemas without taking the database offline. Validated by automatically replaying five years of Wikimedia schema changes. Interests

  • Data Infrastructure
    Particularly interested in the tension between compute and storage disaggregation. While "moving data to compute" and "moving compute to data" are often framed as binary choices, the future lies in an adaptive architecture that balances elasticity with data locality. Partial code shipping — predicate pushdown being the prime example — should be the norm, not the exception.
  • Architecting for AI
    Two halves of one problem: Agents for Systems (LLMs handling debugging, SRE, auto-tuning) and Systems for Agents (infrastructure tuned for agentic workloads). The interesting questions sit at the boundary — what changes about caching, scheduling, and observability when the consumer is an agent that can retry indefinitely.
  • LLMs in Engineering Practice
    How LLMs are reshaping the day-to-day of engineering: code review, debugging, documentation, the small frictions that weren’t worth automating before. Less interested in headline benchmarks; more interested in whether teams are actually faster six months in.
